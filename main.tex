This reflection is guided by two connected frameworks developed across my academic work. The \textbf{Puzzle Plan} is a holistic design methodology that treats complex educational problems as interconnected systems requiring multiple theoretical lenses, community-centered design, iterative refinement, and application across contexts. The \textbf{EQUITAS} framework asks whether classroom systems create equitable, culturally responsive, and accountable pathways for students to access learning, demonstrate competence, and exercise agency, especially those whose ways of learning are often misread by dominant institutions. Together, these frameworks position inclusion not as placement alone, but as the deliberate design of environments in which neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, chronically ill, trauma-experienced, multilingual, disabled, and multiply identified students can participate without erasing difference in order to belong.

\reviewsection{Topics I Now Understand Well}

The topic I understand most strongly after ED452C is that inclusive education is not a place where students are sent; it is a design responsibility. \citet{wehmeyer2021inclusive} framing of special education helped me separate inclusion from simple physical placement. A student can sit in a general education classroom and still be excluded by how directions are delivered, how quickly students are expected to respond, how evidence is collected, or which forms of participation are treated as legitimate. That shift changed my first professional question. Instead of asking, \textit{``What is wrong with this student?''} I now ask, \textit{``What is this environment demanding, and whose thinking is being hidden by that demand?''} I also developed a stronger understanding of disability through a person-environment fit lens. The social-ecological view makes it harder to blame the learner when the real barrier may be speed, language load, sensory demand, executive-function demand, unfamiliar technology, or narrow definitions of competence. In my Pine Brook IGNITE placement, I saw this repeatedly. During hands-on STEM lessons, students who hesitated were not always confused about the content. Some were decoding multi-step verbal directions, negotiating group roles, handling unfamiliar materials, or waiting for a visual cue that made the task make sense. In the AI project brainstorming lesson, ``stuck'' looked similar on the surface even when the barrier was different: a login issue, too many AI-generated choices, uncertainty about connecting a personal interest to computer science, or simple overwhelm. ED452C gave me the language for what I was noticing: \textit{behavior alone does not tell the whole story.}

A second topic I now understand well is self-determination as causal agency. \citet{wehmeyer2013selfdetermination} helped me see self-determination not as a personality trait or a transition-planning milestone, but as something built through repeated experiences of choosing, acting, evaluating, and adjusting. In the AI brainstorming lesson, one student who had been quiet during the verbal setup became more engaged once the task began with a personal-interest choice. He chose a topic connected to a video game he had been playing at home, read the AI output, judged that the first ideas were too complex, and asked the AI to simplify them without being prompted to do so. The key design move was not the technology itself. It was that the student's preference was the entry point; he did not have to earn the right to decide. This matters especially for neurodivergent learners, culturally and linguistically diverse students, multilingual students, disabled students, and students navigating multiple identities. These students are often over-scaffolded or over-directed in the name of support. ED452C helped me name the difference between scaffolding that opens participation and scaffolding that quietly removes agency. Support should reduce unnecessary barriers, but it should not remove the student's opportunity to practice judgment, voice, and authorship.

The third topic I understand more deeply is evidence in inclusive education. My Learning Assistant lesson helped me synthesize this part of the course through the frame of access, participation, growth, and belonging. I no longer regard task completion alone as sufficient. In the Lego sail car lesson at Pine Brook, the build phase consumed the full period, and the redesign phase---the part most connected to student decision-making, testing, revision, and agency---was the first thing lost to time. That helped me understand that evidence is also a design choice. If a lesson only measures who finished fastest, it may miss who revised a strategy, explained an idea to a peer, asked a better question, or needed one different access point to demonstrate what they knew.

My Journal Article Review strengthened this same understanding through the RULER study. \citet{rivers2013ruler} helped me see social-emotional learning as more than individual skill instruction. When implemented well, SEL can reshape classroom climate by increasing warmth, responsiveness, student autonomy, and belonging. That connects directly to my Puzzle Plan and EQUITAS lenses: the environment is part of the intervention. For students with ADHD, chronic illness, trauma histories, anxiety, language differences, or multiple marginalized identities, classroom climate is not secondary to learning. It can determine whether learning feels possible.

\reviewsection{Topics I Want to Learn More About}

The main topic I want to learn more about is how to audit lessons for agency, not only access. Before this course, I often thought first about whether all students could reach the task: \textit{Are the directions clear? Are the materials available? Is there a scaffold? }Those questions still matter, but ED452C pushed me toward a harder question: \textit{Does every student get to be the one who decides?} I want to become better at designing lessons where choice, revision, voice, and self-monitoring are not optional extensions that disappear when time runs short. They need to be built into the core structure of the lesson. I also want to learn more about the balance between scaffolding and over-scaffolding. This is especially important for students who are neurodivergent, multilingual, disabled, or labeled as struggling. Teachers often want to help, but help can become control if it prevents students from practicing judgment. A visual checklist, partner talk structure, or prompt frame can support access without appropriating the student's thinking. But an adult who immediately tells the student what to do next may reduce frustration while simultaneously reducing agency. That tension is something I want to keep investigating. A third topic I want to continue studying is inclusive assessment, especially evidence plans that value process as well as product. I want to better understand how teachers can collect evidence across oral explanation, peer collaboration, revision, debugging talk, planning artifacts, questions, confidence, and belonging over time. This connects to my broader equity work because many students are misread when assessment privileges speed, written output, compliance, or standard academic English. I also want to continue learning how technology and AI can support inclusion without creating new inequities through login barriers, interface complexity, language demands, privacy concerns, or excessive choice.

\reviewsection{How I Will Pursue Further Learning}

I will pursue further learning through practice, documentation, and research. First, I will continue using my teaching placements as sites for testing inclusive design questions in real time. For each lesson I plan or support, I will apply three audit questions before teaching: \textit{What are the likely access barriers? Where is the student's agency built into the task? What evidence will I collect beyond task completion?} These questions are operationally simple enough to apply consistently, yet they connect directly to UDL, strengths-based design, self-determination, and inclusive assessment. Second, I will continue building portfolio-ready documentation from my teaching work. ED452C helped me see that every discussion post, lesson reflection, and placement note can become a piece of a larger professional evidence system. My goal is to de-identify classroom patterns systematically and translate them into reusable teaching evidence---recording not merely what happened, but what the moment revealed about access, agency, participation, and belonging. This practice will strengthen my certification portfolio while also deepening my reflective capacity as a practitioner. Third, I will integrate ED452C into my broader Puzzle Plan and EQUITAS work. My research interests and my teaching interests are not separate projects. In both domains, I am asking how systems define competence, whose evidence is recognized as legitimate, and how design can reduce inequity before harm occurs. In education, that means constructing classrooms where neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, disabled, chronically ill, multilingual, and multiply identified students are not treated as exceptions to be managed. In my larger AI and equity work, it means building systems that do not mistake difference for deficiency. ED452C gave me a practical pedagogical language for the same ethical commitment that drives my research. Going forward, I will continue reading across UDL, self-determination, inclusive assessment, transition planning, culturally sustaining pedagogy, and SEL as classroom-climate design---and translating that reading into repeatable classroom habits: agency audits, evidence-plan checklists, AI prompt routines, student voice structures, and reflection tools that help students name and exercise how they learn.

What I gained from ED452C is ultimately a clearer professional stance: inclusion is not proven by where a student sits, but by what the environment allows the student to access, decide, express, revise, and become. Carrying that stance forward means designing classrooms where students are not merely present, but genuinely recognized as capable, self-determining participants in their own learning.
